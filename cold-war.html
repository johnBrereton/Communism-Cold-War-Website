The Cold War

and the Competion for Global Dominance

The Cold War was marked by an arms race, proxy wars, espionage, and covert operations, and had a profound impact on the international political landscape. It created a bipolar world order, shaped the development of new international institutions, and influenced the formation of alliances and coalitions around the world. The legacy of the Cold War continues to be felt today, as it helped to shape the course of history and the development of societies and cultures around the world ("cold war").

The Cold War commenced following World War II, as the United States and the Soviet Union emerged as two dominant superpowers in the world. The tensions between the two nations were primarily due to contrasting ideologies and political disagreements. These tensions can be traced back to the Yalta Conference in February 1945, where Winston Churchill, Franklin D. Roosevelt, and Joseph Stalin discussed the reorganization of Europe post-war. The United States, being a capitalist democracy, perceived communism's expansion as a threat to national interests and global stability. Conversely, the Soviet Union, being a communist state, perceived the United States as an imperialist aggressor trying to extend its influence and undermine socialist regimes worldwide. The onset of the Cold War created a worldwide division between Western and Eastern blocs, forcing nations worldwide to take sides, having a profound effect on international relations for several decades ("World War").

The arms race during the Cold War was a significant aspect of the geopolitical tension between the United States and the Soviet Union. Both nations invested heavily in the development of nuclear weapons, leading to a buildup of nuclear arsenals that threatened global destruction. The United States was the first to develop an atomic bomb, which was used to devastating effect against Japan in World War II. In 1949, for the first time, the Soviet Union "successfully detonated a nuclear weapon, setting off an arms race that would become a defining feature of the Cold War". This arms race escalated throughout the 1950s and 1960s, with both sides developing more advanced delivery systems, such as intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs) and nuclear submarines. The concept of mutually assured destruction (MAD) emerged, which held that the threat of catastrophic retaliation would prevent either side from launching a first strike. The arms race ultimately resulted in a dangerous and costly nuclear stalemate, with both sides possessing enough weapons to destroy the world several times over (Dorrance).

The space race was another key aspect of the Cold War, as both the United States and the Soviet Union competed to demonstrate their technological superiority and achieve breakthroughs in space exploration. The Sputnik launch "marked the start of the space age and the US-USSR space race, and led to the creation of the National Aeronautics and Space Administration" (Bradley). The Soviet Union had an early advantage, launching the first satellite, Sputnik, in 1957, and sending the first human, Yuri Gagarin, into space in 1961 (“Sputnik”). The United States responded with a massive investment in its space program, culminating in the Apollo moon landing in 1969. The space race was not just a competition between the two superpowers, but also a symbol of their broader ideological conflict. The United States promoted its capitalist system as the key to technological progress and the superiority of its way of life, while the Soviet Union touted communism as the engine of scientific advancement and a superior way of organizing society. Despite the intense rivalry and propaganda, the space race also produced significant scientific and technological advancements, as well as inspiring a generation of young people to pursue careers in science and engineering (Boyle).

Throughout most of the Cold War, "the United States engaged the threat of Soviet expansion through a series of proxy wars". They were a defining features of the Cold War, with both nations using third-party actors to advance their interests without engaging in direct military conflict. The United States, for example, supported the anti-communist government of South Vietnam in the Vietnam War, while the Soviet Union backed communist forces in Angola, Nicaragua, and Afghanistan. The use of proxy wars allowed the superpowers to advance their strategic objectives without the risk of direct military confrontation, but often at the expense of the local populations caught in the middle (Tucker). These conflicts were often highly destabilizing and resulted in significant loss of life and suffering. The proxy wars of the Cold War era were not limited to the military realm but also extended to political, economic, and ideological spheres, with both sides seeking to expand their spheres of influence by supporting friendly governments and movements around the world. Despite the apparent benefits of using proxy wars there were also unintended consequences, such as the rise of militant groups and the proliferation of weapons that continue to destabilize regions to this day (Dorrance).

The Cuban Missile Crisis was a critical moment in the Cold War, which occurred in October 1962. The United States discovered that the Soviet Union had installed nuclear missile bases in Cuba, which posed an existential threat to the United States. President John F. Kennedy demanded that the Soviet Union remove the missiles, and the two nations entered into a tense standoff that brought the world to the brink of nuclear war. For thirteen days, the United States and the Soviet Union engaged in high-stakes diplomacy and military brinkmanship. The United States imposed a naval quarantine around Cuba, preventing the Soviet Union from delivering additional missile supplies, while the Soviet Union increased their military presence in the region. Ultimately, through intense "negotiation and compromise", the Soviet Union agreed to remove their missiles from Cuba in exchange for a US pledge not to invade Cuba and to remove US missiles from Turkey. The Cuban Missile Crisis highlighted the need for diplomatic solutions to international conflicts and underscored the dangers of nuclear brinkmanship (Kiger).

The end of the Cold War began in the late 1980s with a series of political and social changes within the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe. The Soviet economy was struggling, and political reforms under Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev led to greater openness and democratic reforms. This, in turn, emboldened people across Eastern Europe to demand greater political freedoms, which ultimately led to the "collapse of communist regimes across the region" (“The End”). The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 symbolized the end of the Cold War era, and the reunification of Germany was a significant step towards ending the division between East and West. The Soviet Union itself began to unravel, with nationalist movements across the country demanding "greater autonomy and independence". In 1991, a failed coup against Gorbachev led to the formal dissolution of the Soviet Union, and the end of the Cold War. The United States emerged as the sole superpower, and the world entered a new era of international relations. The end of the Cold War brought significant changes to the geopolitical landscape, including the expansion of democracy and market economies, the rise of global terrorism and the continued threat of nuclear weapons (“Collapse of”).
